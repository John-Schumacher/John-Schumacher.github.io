Final Report Deliverables

An example project deliverable of mine.

I place a great deal of effort and pride myself in creating final deliverables that effectively communicate essential results and their resultant recommendations to key stakeholders and all other interested parties. Interestingly, it has been my UX research work where I have genuinely honed this ability to where it is today. Listed below are some of the principles that guide my process of creating deliverables:  

  • Descriptive statistics are intuitively understood better than inferential statistics. Because of this, I find that key findings are often better communicated and conveyed with descriptive statistics. That said,
  • I strive to incorporate inferential findings relevant to critical key recommendations in my deliverables. However, I always try to present them intelligibly and ensure they do not come to dominate my deliverables.
  • A statistic’s numerical form can affect understanding and, consequently, also the ability to communicate and convey findings. Because of this, I always consider how to best present relevant statistics. Humans process and understand:
    • large numbers better than small numbers,
    • whole numbers better than integers,
    • fractions better than decimals, and
    • percentages better than proportions.
  • In my purview, a crucial element of report creation is understanding a few principles about human visual perception preferences and meticulous attention to meeting those preferences. The most critical is that humans are inclined to symmetry over randomness and strongly prefer visual fields possessing coherent geometrical properties allowing for easy scanning. As such, I always strive to deploy elements like font size, font spacing, layout spacing, image size, and color in a consistent hierarchical manner as much as possible to increase perceptions of coherence and convey meaning. 
  • Research suggests that visuals that provide concrete representations are better at compelling interest and conveying numerical information than standard visuals, like charts and graphs. Accordingly, in my deliverables, I will often state the findings’ numerical values and place a visual representation of the physical entity it pertains to next to that value—often using a graphic format like an icon.
  • Because humans have limited processing capabilities and prefer coherent visual scenes, I strive to keep my deliverables from appearing cluttered.
