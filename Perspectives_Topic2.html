Outliers

Datasets often contain abnormal responses/data points,
called outliers. Furthermore, outliers can often affect the final primary test results. Thus, outliers often affect the final recommendations provided to key stakeholders deduced from the results. Consequently, most researchers take a conservative approach (conservative as in prudent, not in the political sense of the word), sometimes even removing all data points they have assessed as an outlier.  

I am not as instinctively cautious with handling outliers as my contemporaries, which is interesting because I tend to be more conservative in most other methodological areas of statistics than my contemporaries. Here, though, with the handling of outliers, I understand being an outlier does not, in-and-of-itself, make the datum terrible. And while, to me, not as egregious as doing nothing and simply ignoring outliers, outlier removal can, and often does, become excessive and detrimental to the goal of accurate representation of a natural phenomenon. 

First, beyond the additional degree of freedom it adds to its model, we know that not every outlier ‘influences’ a statistical model’s final result. And thus, removing every outlier is a bit needless.   Second, and vastly more important, if an outlier influential or not—is valid (i.e., it accurately reflects the value of the construct it attempts to measure), removing it only hinders the final model’s ability to reflect reality accurately. Removing accurate outlier values hinders a model’s ability to reflect reality because one can only safely extrapolate from a model across the measured regions of the constructs under study. And, as outliers often represent the constructs of interests’ extreme values, removing outliers often impairs the ability to extrapolate (i.e., predict) high and low scores accurately.

So, what to do? My approach is to search for outliers and other responses that can be safely classified as careless responses (i.e., non-responses). Careless responses are those answered without concern to its item’s content and, accordingly, do not reflect the actual level of the construct being measured. Because careless responses are often also outliers, outlier detection analysis is one of their many sound detection techniques. When I discover outliers, however, I take great care and effort to assess the plausibility of the response. If I determine that the response is plausible but unique, I am more likely to leave it in the model than remove it. However, if the model’s significance rests on the outlier responses that I deem reasonable and accurate, I will report both results to stakeholders in an intelligible manner.
